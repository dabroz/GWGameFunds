## Who's Who

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Developer (dev)** | The studio or person making the game. |
| **Publisher** | A company that funds and/or brings a game to market: marketing, QA, localization, porting, platform relations. Takes a revenue share in return. |
| **Investor** | Anyone putting money in expecting a financial return — in a single game (*project investment*) or in your company (*equity investment*). |
| **VC (Venture Capital)** | A fund that invests in companies (equity) expecting a few big wins to pay for many losses. VCs invest in *studios*, rarely in single games. |
| **Angel investor** | An individual investing their own money, usually earlier and in smaller amounts than a VC. |
| **Platform holder** | Steam (Valve), PlayStation (Sony), Xbox (Microsoft), Nintendo, Epic, Apple, Google. Some run their own funding programs (see [section D of the directory](GameFundingDirectory.md#d-aaa-publishers--platform-programs)). |
| **Grant body** | A government agency, EU program or foundation giving *non-dilutive* money (you don't give up ownership or revenue). See [section G](GameFundingDirectory.md#g-grants--public-funding). |
| **Developer-run fund** | A fund created by a successful studio to finance other devs, often on friendlier terms (see [section F](GameFundingDirectory.md#f-developer-run-funds--alternative-financing)). |

## Production Stages & Pitch Materials

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Prototype** | Ugly-but-playable proof that the core mechanic is fun. Made to answer one question: "does this work?" |
| **Vertical slice** | A short, polished section of the game showing final quality across all disciplines (art, audio, UI, gameplay). The standard proof for publisher pitches. |
| **Demo** | A public-facing playable slice, e.g. for Steam Next Fest. Different goal than a vertical slice: player acquisition, not funding. |
| **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)** | The smallest version of the game that delivers its core promise. |
| **Pitch deck** | A short presentation (usually 10–20 slides) selling your game/studio to a funder. See the [Pitch Deck Tutorial](PitchDeckTutorial.md). |
| **One-pager** | A single-page summary of the game — often requested before a full deck. |
| **Game Design Document (GDD)** | The full design reference. Funders rarely read it, but may ask for it during due diligence. |
| **Milestone** | A contractual deliverable ("alpha build with 10 levels by March"). Publisher/investor money is usually paid out *per milestone*, not up front. |
| **Alpha / Beta / Gold** | Feature-complete (alpha) → content-complete and bug-fixing (beta) → release version (gold). Definitions vary by contract — **define them in yours**. |

## Publishing Deal Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Advance** | Money the publisher pays during development. Almost always **recoupable** — it's a loan against your future revenue share, not a gift. |
| **Recoup(ment)** | The publisher takes 100% (or an agreed share) of revenue until the advance (and often marketing costs) is paid back. Only then does your rev share start flowing. |
| **Revenue share (rev share)** | How income is split after recoup, e.g. 70/30 dev/publisher. Everything is negotiable — including whether the split changes after recoup. |
| **Minimum Guarantee (MG)** | A guaranteed minimum payment to the dev regardless of sales. More common in porting/licensing deals. |
| **Gross vs Net revenue** | Gross = all money in. Net = after platform cut (~30%), taxes, refunds, and *whatever else the contract sneaks in*. Always ask what "net" means — this is where deals go bad. |
| **Cross-collateralization** | Recouping one game's advance from *another* game's (or platform's) revenue. Dangerous for the dev — one flop can eat your hit. Avoid or limit it. |
| **IP (Intellectual Property) rights** | Who owns the game, its name, characters, world. The single most important clause. Keeping your IP means keeping sequels, merch, film rights and your studio's long-term value. |
| **Sequel rights / Right of first refusal (ROFR)** | The publisher gets first shot at funding the sequel, or an outright option on it. Limits your future freedom — negotiate scope and expiry. |
| **Exclusivity** | Platform exclusivity (e.g. Epic exclusive) or publisher exclusivity for a period. Usually paid for — make sure it's paid *enough*. |
| **Termination clause** | Under what conditions each side can walk away, and who keeps what (money, IP, builds) when it happens. |
| **Work-for-hire** | You build someone else's game for a fee. No IP, no rev share, but predictable income. Many studios use it to survive between own projects. |

## Investment & Equity Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Equity** | Ownership share in your *company*. Selling equity = selling a piece of everything you'll ever make, not just one game. |
| **Valuation** | What your company is worth. *Pre-money* = before the investment, *post-money* = after (pre-money + cash invested). A $1M investment at $4M pre-money buys 20% of the company. |
| **Dilution** | Your ownership percentage shrinking as new shares are issued in later rounds. Normal — but model it before you sign. |
| **Cap table** | The spreadsheet of who owns what percentage of the company. Investors will ask for it. |
| **Term sheet** | Non-binding summary of a proposed deal's key terms. Signing one usually starts exclusivity and due diligence. |
| **Due diligence** | The investor's audit of your company: finances, contracts, IP ownership, team. Have your paperwork clean *before* you pitch. |
| **SAFE / Convertible note** | Investment instruments that convert into equity at a later round, postponing the valuation debate. Common for early-stage studios. |
| **Seed / Series A/B…** | Funding round stages. Seed = first institutional money (often $0.5–3M for studios); Series A and beyond = scaling. |
| **Exit** | How investors get their money back: acquisition of the studio, or (rarely in games) IPO. If you take VC money, an exit is the *expected* outcome — be sure you want that. |
| **Vesting** | Founder shares are earned over time (typically 4 years). Protects everyone if a co-founder leaves early. |
| **Liquidation preference** | On exit, investors get paid first (usually 1× their money) before common shareholders. Standard, but watch for multiples above 1×. |
| **Non-dilutive funding** | Money that costs no equity and no rev share — grants, tax incentives, some platform deals. The form of financing with the lowest cost of capital, so it's usually worth pursuing first. |

## Money & Metrics

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Budget** | Full cost of making *and shipping* the game — including salaries (yes, yours too), tax, marketing, porting, localization and a contingency buffer (10–20%). |
| **Burn rate** | How much money your studio spends per month. |
| **Runway** | How many months you can survive at current burn: cash ÷ burn rate. |
| **ROI (Return on Investment)** | Profit relative to money invested. What project investors care about most. |
| **Comparables ("comps")** | Released games similar to yours in genre/scope/price, used to estimate your sales potential. The backbone of every pitch's market slide. |
| **Wishlists** | Steam wishlist count — the industry's favorite pre-launch traction metric. Rough rule of thumb: first-week sales ≈ 10–20% of launch wishlists (varies a lot). |
| **CCU** | Concurrent users — players online at the same time. Key metric for multiplayer/GaaS pitches. |
| **Retention (D1/D7/D30)** | Percentage of players returning after 1/7/30 days. Critical for F2P/mobile pitches. |
| **Conversion** | Demo→purchase or wishlist→purchase rate. |
| **GaaS (Games as a Service)** | Games designed for years of post-launch content and monetization. Funders evaluate these on retention and monetization, not just launch sales. |
| **Gap financing** | Money covering the *gap* between what you have (grant, savings, partial publisher deal) and the full budget. |
| **Tax incentive / rebate** | Government programs returning a percentage of qualified dev spend (e.g. the UK's VGTR/VGEC or France's CIJV). A non-dilutive way to recover part of the budget for eligible studios. |
